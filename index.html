Conflict Resolution — The Hierarchy You Must Know

The PMI-preferred conflict resolution technique depends on the situation. Memorize this priority order:

1st Choice Always
Collaborate / Problem Solve — find a win-win. PMI loves this.
When time is short
Compromise / Reconcile — both sides give something up.
When issue is minor
Smooth / Accommodate — acknowledge disagreement, downplay it.
Temporary relief only
Withdraw / Avoid — never first choice, never a solution.
Last resort
Force / Direct — lose-lose for relationship. Only when critical deadline.
  • The exam often makes "force" sound urgent and reasonable — it's almost never right
  • If the question says "the project manager has authority" — still pick collaborate first
  • Smoothing ≠ ignoring — it's acknowledging then de-emphasizing
Leadership Styles — Situational Leadership
Directing
Low skill/low will. Tell them exactly what to do. New team members.
Coaching
High will/low skill. Explain and encourage. Person is motivated but inexperienced.
Supporting
High skill/low will. Share decisions, boost motivation. Experienced but disengaged.
Delegating
High skill/high will. Step back. Fully capable team member.
Servant Leader
Agile default. Remove impediments. Focus on team needs. NOT passive.
Laissez-Faire
Hands-off. Good for expert teams. PMI treats this carefully — not always negative.
  • Agile PM is ALWAYS servant leader by default unless told otherwise
  • "Servant leader" does NOT mean being passive — it means enabling the team
  • Telling a senior expert what to do = micromanaging = wrong answer
Motivation Theories — Quick Reference
Maslow's Hierarchy
Physical → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualization. Lowest unmet need dominates.
Herzberg Two-Factor
Hygiene factors (salary, safety) prevent dissatisfaction. Motivators (recognition, achievement) drive satisfaction. Fixing hygiene ≠ motivation.
McGregor X/Y
Theory X: people avoid work, need control. Theory Y: people are self-motivated. PMI prefers Y.
McClelland
Achievement, Affiliation, Power. Most managers driven by power (institutional, not personal).
Expectancy Theory (Vroom)
Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence. People work harder if they believe effort leads to reward they value.
  • Giving someone a raise (hygiene) doesn't motivate — it only removes dissatisfaction
  • "Give recognition" is almost always right for motivation questions
Agile Team Dynamics (Tuckman, Roles, Self-Organization)
Tuckman Stages
Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning. Conflict = Storming, normal and necessary.
Scrum Master vs PM
SM is servant leader, coach, impediment remover. NOT a traditional PM. Does NOT assign tasks.
Self-Organizing Teams
Team decides HOW to do work. PM/SM decides WHAT. Never assign tasks to individuals in agile.
Cross-Functional
Team has all skills needed. No external dependencies for core work.
Team Charter
Team creates their own working agreements. PM facilitates, doesn't dictate.
Retrospective Purpose
Improve process. Blame-free. Team-led. Continuous improvement, not post-mortem.
🧠 Mini Practice Questions — Resource
Q: Two team members are in a heated disagreement about the technical approach. The project manager should FIRST:
A. Escalate to the sponsor for a decision
B. Meet with both team members to facilitate a collaborative discussion
C. Make the technical decision for them to keep the project moving
D. Ask them to document their positions and vote
Problem Solve / Collaborate is always the first technique. Escalating or forcing decisions skips the step of empowering the team.
Q: An experienced senior engineer seems disengaged and is delivering below his capability. The PM should:
A. Assign a mentor to coach him on task execution
B. Have a one-on-one to understand his concerns and involve him in decisions
C. Document the performance issue and discuss at the next team meeting
D. Provide more detailed task instructions
High skill / low will = Supporting style. You involve them in decisions and address what's killing motivation. Coaching (A) is for low skill. Directing (D) is micromanaging an expert.
Q: In an agile project, a sprint has started. A team member asks the Scrum Master who they should work with first. The SM should:
A. Assign tasks based on skills to maximize velocity
B. Remind the team they are self-organizing and should pull work from the backlog
C. Check the sprint plan and direct the team member accordingly
D. Escalate to the Product Owner for prioritization
Self-organizing teams pull work — they are not assigned. The SM's role is to remind and enable, not direct. PO prioritizes the backlog, not individual tasks.

Change Control — Predictive vs Agile Approach

PREDICTIVE — Formal CCB

  • All changes go through Change Control Board
  • Never implement changes without approval
  • PM submits change request, CCB decides
  • Approved → update baseline, plan, and docs
  • Corrective action = approved change
  • Emergency changes still need documentation after

AGILE — Embrace Change

  • Change welcomed via backlog refinement
  • PO reprioritizes — no formal CCB
  • Changes go INTO the backlog, not change requests
  • Team capacity is fixed; scope flexes
  • Sprint scope is frozen once sprint starts
  • Never change sprint scope mid-sprint (unless critical)
  • If predictive: NEVER approve your own changes as PM (CCB decides)
  • If agile: stakeholder requests go to PO, not directly to the team
  • Hybrid: use CCB for scope/budget baseline; agile backlog for feature changes
Project Charter & Initiating — What PM Does First
Charter Purpose
Formally authorizes the project. Gives PM authority. Signed by sponsor, not PM.
PM Authority
Comes from the charter. Without it, PM has limited power over resources.
Lessons Learned
Review historical lessons FIRST before planning. Apply them throughout, not just closeout.
Kickoff Meeting
End of planning, beginning of execution. Align team on plan and objectives.
Integrated Change
Every approved change must update ALL affected docs: scope, schedule, cost, risk.
Agile Integration
PM acts as integrator across teams. Uses Scrum of Scrums for multi-team coordination.
  • If asked what to do FIRST on a new project: review OPA/EEF and lessons learned
  • Sponsor closes the project — not the PM (formally)
  • PM can make corrective action in execution, but must document it as a change
🧠 Mini Practice — Integration
Q: A stakeholder requests a significant feature change during a sprint. As the Scrum Master, you should:
A. Add it to the current sprint if the team has capacity
B. Direct the stakeholder to the Product Owner to assess and add to the product backlog
C. Submit a formal change request to the CCB
D. Reject the change and ask them to wait for the next release
In agile, all change requests flow through the Product Owner. Sprint scope is protected. The PO will prioritize it in the backlog for a future sprint.

The Core EVM Formulas — Must Be Instant Recall
Variances (negative = BAD)
CV = EV – AC     SV = EV – PV
Indices (>1.0 = GOOD, <1.0 = BAD)
CPI = EV / AC     SPI = EV / PV
EAC Variants (know which to use when)
EAC = BAC / CPI               ← if current variance is TYPICAL
EAC = AC + (BAC – EV)         ← if variance is ATYPICAL (one-time)
EAC = AC + [(BAC–EV)/(CPI×SPI)]   ← if both cost AND schedule matter
ETC & VAC
ETC = EAC – AC     VAC = BAC – EAC
TCPI (work efficiency needed going forward)
TCPI = (BAC – EV) / (BAC – AC)    ← to meet original budget
TCPI = (BAC – EV) / (EAC – AC)    ← to meet new EAC
  • TCPI > 1.0 means you must work MORE efficiently than you have been — often unrealistic
  • SPI can return to 1.0 near project end even if you were late (misleading)
  • EV is always "the budgeted cost of work performed" — not what you actually spent
  • If the question says variance is "atypical" use EAC = AC + (BAC-EV)
Reading EVM Situations Fast
Over budget, on schedule
CV < 0, SV = 0. CPI < 1, SPI = 1. Spending too much per unit of work.
Under budget, behind schedule
CV > 0, SV < 0. CPI > 1, SPI < 1. Doing less work than planned but cheaply.
Best position
CV > 0 AND SV > 0. Under budget AND ahead of schedule.
Worst position
CV < 0 AND SV < 0. Over budget AND behind schedule.
🧠 Mini Practice — EVM
Q: PV=$500K, EV=$400K, AC=$480K. The project is:
A. Behind schedule and over budget
B. Ahead of schedule and under budget
C. Behind schedule and under budget
D. On schedule and over budget
SV = EV-PV = 400-500 = -100 (behind schedule). CV = EV-AC = 400-480 = -80 (over budget). Both negative = worst quadrant.
Q: BAC=$1M, EV=$600K, AC=$700K. The cost overrun was due to a one-time equipment failure. EAC = ?
A. $1,166,667 (BAC/CPI)
B. $1,100,000 (AC + BAC – EV)
C. $700,000
D. $1,400,000
"One-time" / "atypical" variance → use EAC = AC + (BAC–EV) = 700K + (1M–600K) = 700K + 400K = $1.1M. The keyword "atypical" always flags this formula.

Agile Scheduling Concepts
Velocity
Story points completed per sprint. Based on historical data. NOT a commitment — a forecast.
Release Planning
Total backlog story points ÷ velocity = sprints needed. Always an estimate.
Definition of Done
Team-agreed criteria for "complete." Prevents scope creep within a story. Consistent across team.
Definition of Ready
Story is sufficiently defined to begin. INVEST criteria: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable.
Burndown Chart
Remaining work vs. time. Going DOWN is good. Flat line = blocked. Going up = scope added.
Burnup Chart
Work completed vs. total. Shows both progress AND scope changes. Better for communicating scope creep.
  • If velocity drops: first inspect WHY in the retrospective, then adjust the plan
  • Never commit to delivering specific stories until sprint planning is done with the team
  • Scope in agile is flexible — but you never add to a sprint mid-sprint without removing something
Agile Scope Control (M&C)
  • Scope validation = stakeholder acceptance. In agile, this happens every sprint review.
  • If a stakeholder says delivered work doesn't meet expectations at sprint review → refine and add to backlog, don't fail the sprint
  • Product backlog is NEVER complete — it evolves continuously
  • Backlog refinement / grooming happens continuously (not just at sprint start)
  • Scope baseline in agile = the product vision + prioritized backlog (not a fixed WBS)

Communications
4 MISSES

Key Concepts
Communication Channels
n(n-1)/2  where n = number of people
  • Communication models: Sender → Encode → Message → Channel → Decode → Receiver → Feedback
  • Noise can corrupt any step. PM's job: confirm receipt AND understanding
  • Most communication problems = PM not communicating proactively
  • Formal written: contractual changes, legal issues, scope changes
  • Informal verbal: day-to-day team coordination
  • In agile: face-to-face is preferred (information radiators, standups)
  • Communications management plan: who gets what info, when, in what format
  • If a stakeholder says they "didn't know" about something → PM failed to communicate, even if it was in a report
  • 55% nonverbal, 38% tone, 7% words (Mehrabian) — PMP loves to test this

Procurement
4 MISSES
Qs: 67, 124, 126, 153
Key Concepts
FFP (Firm Fixed Price)
Seller bears cost risk. Best when scope is well-defined.
CPFF (Cost Plus Fixed Fee)
Buyer bears cost risk. Seller gets costs + fixed fee. Good for uncertain scope.
T&M (Time & Material)
Hybrid. Buyer bears risk of efficiency. Good for staff augmentation. Must include not-to-exceed clause.
CPIF (Cost Plus Incentive Fee)
Buyer bears risk but incentivizes seller to control costs. Shared savings.
  • Changes to a contract must ALWAYS be in writing — verbal agreements don't count
  • Contract disputes → negotiate first, then ADR (mediation/arbitration), then litigation
  • Centralized procurement: PM shares contractor with PMO. Decentralized: PM fully controls contractor.
  • Contract closeout comes BEFORE administrative closeout

🎯 Exam Day Decision Framework
Apply to Every Question

When stuck, run this mental checklist:

  • Does it involve people? → Prefer collaborate, support, and empathize over authority or escalation
  • Is it agile? → PO owns scope decisions. Team self-organizes. SM removes impediments.
  • Is it predictive? → Follow the process. CCB for changes. Document everything.
  • What do you do FIRST? → Usually: assess the situation, then communicate, then plan the response
  • Who should make this decision? → Lowest possible level. Don't escalate what can be resolved at team level.
  • Is it proactive or reactive? → PMI ALWAYS prefers proactive. If an answer prevents a problem vs fixes one, pick prevent.
  • EVM question? → Write out EV, PV, AC before doing any math. One number wrong cascades.
